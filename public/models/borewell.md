# The Borewell Game

In many parts of the world, communities share a single underground
aquifer — a giant natural water tank formed over thousands of years.
Each household decides for itself how much to pump.

## What to try

- Run the simulation with default settings. What happens to the aquifer
  level over 120 months (10 years)?
- Increase the **pumping rate** to maximum. How quickly does the aquifer
  collapse?
- Turn off the **recharge** flow. How does losing rainfall recharge
  change the rate of depletion?
- Reduce pumping to a level where the aquifer stays stable. What is that
  level, given the default rainfall?
- Increase **rainfall** until the aquifer is sustainable at high pumping.

## Things to notice

The aquifer doesn't just fall at a constant rate — the *acceleration*
matters. Notice how the system behaves differently when recharge is on
versus off.

The tipping point: there is a pumping rate at which the aquifer is
exactly stable. Above it, collapse is inevitable regardless of how long
you wait.

## Discussion questions

1. If each individual household pumps just a little more, what happens
   to the shared aquifer? Who is responsible for the outcome?
2. What would need to change — rules, prices, technology — for the
   system to be sustainable?
3. In the real world, what slows down the feedback between aquifer
   depletion and behaviour change?
